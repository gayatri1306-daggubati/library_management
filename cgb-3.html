DARE TO LEAD

~ Brene Brown

Courageous Leadership Requires Vulnerability

One of the core themes in Dare to Lead is that true leadership is built on courage, and at the heart of courage lies vulnerability. Brené Brown defines vulnerability not as weakness but as the ability to take risks, be emotionally exposed, and embrace uncertainty. Many people assume that strong leaders should never show doubt, fear, or emotion. However, Brown argues that the opposite is true—leaders who are willing to be vulnerable build deeper trust, inspire innovation, and create meaningful connections within their teams.

Vulnerability in leadership means having difficult conversations, admitting mistakes, and being open to feedback. A leader who avoids vulnerability may struggle to address challenges honestly, leading to poor communication and disengagement among employees. Brown introduces the concept of rumbling with vulnerability, which means actively engaging in honest, difficult discussions rather than avoiding them.

To practice vulnerability, leaders must:

  • Create psychological safety – Encourage open dialogue and make it safe for employees to share their thoughts.
  • Lead with empathy – Understand the emotions behind actions rather than reacting defensively.
  • Normalize failure as part of growth – Encourage learning from mistakes instead of fearing them.

Clear is Kind, Unclear is Unkind

Another powerful lesson from Dare to Lead is the idea that clarity is one of the greatest acts of kindness a leader can offer. Brown explains that many leaders avoid direct and honest communication because they fear conflict or don’t want to hurt someone’s feelings. However, failing to be clear about expectations, feedback, or goals can lead to confusion, frustration, and poor performance.

Brown emphasizes that avoiding hard conversations does not prevent discomfort—it just delays it and makes things worse. Leaders who are afraid to give clear feedback often end up dealing with bigger issues later, such as unresolved conflicts, unmet expectations, or employee disengagement.

To practice clear communication, leaders should:

  • Be specific and direct – Avoid vague feedback; instead, give actionable guidance.
  • Set clear expectations – Ensure everyone knows their roles, responsibilities, and goals.
  • Address problems early – Don’t wait for small misunderstandings to grow into bigger issues.
